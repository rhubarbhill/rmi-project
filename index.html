Historical Overview of Dr. J. Marion Sims

Explore the life and legacy of the controversial figure in modern gynecology.

In the modern day, Dr. J. Marion Sims (1813-1883, pictured) is widely recognized as the "father of modern gynecology." He is most famously credited with pioneering the first successful and repeatable surgical technique to repair vesico-vaginal fistulas (VVFs) among women. VVF was a complication caused by obstructed labor that led to urinary and fecal incontinence. Any women who had it were often socially ostracized for it, sometimes committing suicide.

Among the surgical community at the time, it was considered an incurable condition, and Sims had to undertake a lot of medical experimentation over multiple years in his attempt to find a way to reliably repair VVF. He developed a technique that used silver sutures which could successfully close the fistula and cure women permanently.

This technique, however, was developed through many failed attempts at surgery on many enslaved women, including three very critical women named Anarcha, Betsey, and Lucy. These were essentially surgical experiments, performed without anesthesia and initially with other surgeons watching as the patient experienced excruciating pain, perpetuating the idea that black women were more resistant to pain. By modern ethical standards, "consent" could not have been obtained and the women were treated more as medical subjects than autonomous human beings.

After his successful stint with curing VVF, he went on to enjoy a fruitful and fulfilling medical career, founding the New York Women's Hospital and serving as president of the American Medical Association in 1876. His innovations in gynecology became standard practice and he was internationally recognized (see the contemporary news column to the left). However, he was silent about the enslaved women who helped him make his innovations, omitting to mention them at all in his initial publications about fistula treatment and minimizing their role and suffering in later speeches.

Owing to his accomplishments and likeable character amongst his colleagues, his friends raised money to have a statue made of him following his death. It was first placed in New York City's Bryant Park in October 1894 then later moved to Central Park. However, in 2018, as modern society re-assessed his legacy so entangled with that of slavery, his statue was removed and taken to Sims' burial ground in Brooklyn's Green Wood Ceremony.

He is an undeniably important figure in gynecology, but his work is inseparable from the racial and ethical injustices embedded in his methods. Some authors, such as Prof. L. Lewis Wall, have argued that Sims' actions cannot be judged appropriately by modern standards, while others, such as Prof. Durrenda Ojanuga and Prof. Rueben C. Warren, believe that he undeniably violated ethical and medical standards even at the time.
